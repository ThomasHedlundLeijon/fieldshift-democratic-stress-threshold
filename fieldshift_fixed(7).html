The Hedlund-Leijon Principle

This asymmetry is stable across time periods, regions, and datasets — and yields a predictive threshold at κ = 0.216.

The Hedlund-Leijon Constant

The constant ℋ is derived from the ratio of cumulative democratic recovery to cumulative democratic erosion across all country-year observations in the V-Dem dataset.

ℋ = Σ(positive ΔD) / Σ|negative ΔD| = 109.38 / 63.46 = 1.7236
For country i at year t: ΔD(i,t) = D(i,t) − D(i,t−1). Positive changes = recovery; negative changes = erosion. Minimum change threshold: 0.01 (1 percentage point), following Lührmann & Lindberg 2019. Sensitivity analysis: thresholds 0.005–0.015 produce estimates within ±0.03 of the central value.

The Critical Threshold κ = 0.216

Derived from the geometry of a sine wave: the point of maximum slope — steepest acceleration — occurs at π/4 radians = 1/8 of the full 2π cycle. Scaled by ℋ, this yields the early-warning threshold. Below κ = 0.216 lies the Autocracy Pit. Between 0.216–0.35 is the Risk Zone. Between 0.35–0.70 is the Warning Zone. Above 0.70 is the Democracy Valley.

κ = ℋ / 8 = 1.7236 / 8 = 0.2154 ≈ 0.216
Below this score, the system enters a zone of increased acceleration toward autocracy. Recovery remains possible — Poland (2023) demonstrates this — but requires extraordinary political mobilisation, international intervention, or fundamental institutional rebuilding.

The Hedlund-Leijon Wave Model

A stylized dynamic model capturing oscillation, path dependence, damping, and asymmetry. Parameters: ℋ = 1.724, κ = 0.216, T ≈ 50 years. Not a deterministic prediction for individual countries — a representation of general structural tendency.

D(t) = D_eq + A₀ · e^(−δt) · sin(ℋ · t / T + φ)
D(t) = democracy level  ·  D_eq = long-run equilibrium  ·  A₀ = amplitude  ·  δ = damping
ℋ = 1.724  ·  T ≈ 50 years (primary cycle)  ·  φ = phase shift

Election Math
Mathematical triangulation · Pre-election forecasting · Post-election validation

Election Math is the FieldShift module for turning structural democracy data into testable quantitative forecasts. It combines eight variables — polling, electoral system mechanics, diaspora and institutional bias, district geometry, turnout differentials, prediction markets, HL structural risk, and historical incumbent performance — into a forecast that is published, date-locked, and checked against the actual result after election day.

What you learn
Why polls alone are not enough
In Hungary 2022, polls showed Fidesz +5 points. The actual result was +20. Election Math teaches you why — and how to correct for it using the Structural Amplification Factor (V2).
What it produces
A locked, dated prediction
The model generates a documented prediction with confidence intervals — vote share, seat distribution, majority type — published before polling day so it can be scientifically validated after.
Why it matters
Structural vs surface analysis
FieldShift adds what no poll can: the V-Dem democratic health score, the HL erosion trajectory, and the institutional risk profile. This tells you what the election means for democracy — regardless of who wins.
The 8 triangulation variables
Scientific purpose: Every Election Math prediction is locked with a date stamp before the election and compared against the actual result afterward. This is how forecasting models are scientifically validated.
